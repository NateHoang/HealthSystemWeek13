  1. First – what is Policy? In general, it refers to the rules or norms that dictate how we do things. People sometimes separate policy into big “P” policy and little “p” policy.
  2. Big “P” policy refers to formal rules including laws, regulations, formal directives (e.g. Provider Bulletins), official interpretations (e.g. CMS/State Guidance), unofficial interpretations (e.g. phone call, audit), and contracts.
  3. Little “p” policies are more informal and can include things like informal or internal guidelines (e.g. dress code), workflows, or informal agreements. We will talk about both kinds of policies in considering how the idea of new delivery systems is being supported or inhibited by policies.

Page 6

  1. Let’s start with this question: Can a dentist make a diagnosis and treatment plan by reviewing telehealth records without performing an in-person examination? After several decades doing this kind of work, reviewing the literature, and following official and informal interpretations and policy adoption, I can say that the answer is clearly yes.

Page 7

  1. We did a structured study of this issue which was published in the article shown here. We compared dentist decision making after reviewing telehealth records versus after reviewing telehealth records plus an in-person examination. We found that dentists made essentially the same decisions with or without the in-person examination.
  2. There was some small variation between the decisions made by a single dentist. However, that variation was very small compared to the variation among multiple dentists. Given that all the dentists in the study were dental school faculty, we concluded that dentists could, in fact make decisions without an in-person examination that were well within the acceptable variation among dentists.

  1. There have now been many published studies that have come to the same conclusion such as the study depicted here.

  1. The definition of a dental examination contained in the CDT Codes, the official code set used in dental practice, state that the dentist responsibility is to interpret data and make a diagnosis and treatment plan. However, the dentist does not need to personally collect the data.

Page 10

  1. In addition to the studies mentioned earlier, we now have decades of experience with dentists performing virtual examinations and increasingly comfortable doing that.
  2. What helps some dentist become comfortable doing telehealth exams is the realization that there is typically a range of findings. At one end of the range are patients who are clearly healthy, and it is obvious that there is no point in having the individual travel to a dental office to reconfirm what is already clear from the telehealth exam. At the other end of the range are patients who have a lot of disease and it is clear that they need to go to a dental office. So the concerns are really only about a subset of patients in the middle. We find that as dentists get more comfortable with remote examinations that gray area in the middle gets smaller. It is also helpful to realize that a dentist does not have to make a final decision for every situation. They always have the option to decide that they cannot decide and ask that the patient come to the office to complete the exam. Again, as dentists get more comfortable doing remote examinations, they tend to have fewer patients come for an examination in-person.
